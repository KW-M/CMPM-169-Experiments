Experiment 6 - A how-to guide

In this experiment I played with tracery grammars and ai language models. I was inspired by the Retro Encabulator to make a tracery grammar that can make formal assembly instructions for wacky high-tech sounding devices. I also took a book from the current chatbot trend to turn the instructions into a poorly constructed, broken, but sometimes funny informational chatbot service using the co:here large language model api.

* WARNING: IT RARELY MAY GENERATE OFFENSIVE CONTENT. PLEASE DO NOT TREAT REPLIES SERIOUSLY! *

Imitate

First I took tracery's recursive story examples and applying them to basic assembly instructions. The results were good but rarely gramatically correct or remotely sensible. To marginally improve the coherence of the instructions I used tracery's state saving features to keep track of which parts were used in which steps and resuse the same part names in later sections.

Integrate

I had just finished another tool using co:here AI text for custom recipe generation called Pantry Chef. I thought it would be fun to feed the crude, generated output from tracery into the "smarter" large language model, and ask it to explain the device in question.

Innovate

Having the ai generate responses was fun as it generally made for strange, tanjential, but kind of coherent answers explaining all manner of things about these completely made up devices. To put it all together I opted for the old chatbot style of having limited questions in button bubbles that continue the conversation, except I left the ai hanging, so it always sounds bad in the end, just like mid 2010's chatbots were like.

Reflection

I enjoyed the challenge of writing tracery grammars that are remotely sensible. Although I certainly had alot of leway with my topic of choice. Seing how the ai responds is still quite fun, and entertaining, and I hope you can enjoy it as well.
